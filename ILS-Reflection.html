Reflection

I think this internship was a very beneficial experience. Being a lab that allowed me to work with both life sciences and computer science (my two majors), I feel that I was able to incorporate all the knowledge I had been learning from all my courses. I became much more familiar with coding while also being able to see how I could apply my CS knowledge into the biology world.

When first joining, I was nervous that I would be a weird in-between: an undergraduate who knew a bit about biology and a bit about CS. As I had not chosen one to focus on, I felt I exceled at neither. Initially, I would attend daily meetings and hear from members of the team, feeling as if I could not understand either side, despite supposedly being knowledgable in both. However, my mentor quickly guided me in, lending me papers to read and tutorials to watch.

As I tried to adapt, I quickly realized how special my role was on the team. Sure, I was the most inexperienced (and newest) member in the lab, but as I met the other people in the lab, I realized that many of them were very specialized towards eihter Biology or CS, with only minor experience in the other and that I could help play a pivotal role in bridging the gap between the two. As my internship progressed and I started to understand and learn from both sides, my role settled and I was able to contribute a lot to the team. Some of the researchers would ask if we could design code that could help them interpret specific types of data from the lab, and I served as someone who could explain to the other software engineers what it is they desired.

Although I am still unsure of which direction I want to take, I think this research experience was very beneficial. It taught me something that classes could have never, which is how important it is to communicate while working on a team, and that regardless of which field I decide on, I believe I can carry these communicative skills with me.
